Overview

The Air Quality Life Index (AQLI), developed by the University of Chicago's Energy Policy Institute (EPIC), quantifies the impact of air pollution on life expectancy. By leveraging hyper-local data, the AQLI enables users worldwide to explore how reducing particulate pollution to meet World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines, national standards, or custom targets could extend life expectancy. This powerful tool informs communities and policymakers about the profound benefits of air quality improvements, measured in the most critical metric: longer, healthier lives.

Led by Milton Friedman Distinguished Service Professor in Economics Michael Greenstone, the AQLI is grounded in rigorous, peer-reviewed research that establishes a causal link between long-term exposure to particulate matter (PM₂.₅) and reduced life expectancy. It is a cornerstone of EPIC's Clean Air Program, dedicated to providing actionable air quality insights globally.

Understanding Air Pollution

Particulate matter (PM₂.₅), a primary focus of the AQLI, is predominantly human-generated, with natural sources such as dust and wildfires excluded from analyses. Major sources of PM₂.₅ include:

  • Fossil Fuel Combustion: Coal-fired power plants and industrial facilities emit sulfur dioxide, forming sulfate particulates. High-temperature combustion in vehicles and power plants produces nitrogen dioxide, yielding nitrate particulates. Incomplete combustion in diesel engines and household coal use generates black carbon, a key contributor to climate change.
  • Biofuel Burning: Household cooking and heating with wood or crop residue release black carbon and organic particulates, often matching fossil fuel impacts in certain regions.
  • Biomass Burning: Agricultural practices, such as burning forests or crop residue, significantly contribute to particulate pollution.

The interconnection between air pollution and climate change is evident, as fossil fuel combustion releases both PM₂.₅ and greenhouse gases. Addressing air pollution not only improves public health but also mitigates climate change, promoting global sustainability.

Methodology

The AQLI employs a robust methodology to estimate the relationship between air pollution and life expectancy, integrating data from peer-reviewed studies, global population statistics, and high-resolution PM₂.₅ measurements. It calculates potential life expectancy gains if air quality aligns with WHO guidelines (5 µg/m³ for PM₂.₅), national standards, or user-defined targets.

Key components include:

  • Research Foundation: Built on studies by Chen et al. (2013) and Ebenstein et al. (2017), which utilized China's Huai River policy to quantify the impact of sustained PM₂.₅ exposure on life expectancy.
  • Data Sources: Incorporates satellite-derived PM₂.₅ data from the Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group (ACAG) and van Donkelaar et al. (2021), providing granular air quality insights.
  • Calculations: Combines pollution data with epidemiological models to estimate life expectancy losses and potential gains from pollution reduction.
  • Policy Scenarios: Enables users to explore the life expectancy benefits of various air quality standards, offering actionable insights for policy development.

For a detailed methodology, visit the AQLI Methodology page.

Global Impact

Hailed as the "gold standard on global air quality research" by The New York Times and a "World Changing Idea" by Fast Company, the AQLI has reached over 1 billion people across 20 languages through extensive media coverage. Its data has driven significant policy changes worldwide:

  • Indian Members of Parliament cited AQLI data to support amendments to the Indian Air Act.
  • A landmark Indonesian court ruling referenced AQLI data to mandate stronger air quality protections in Jakarta.
  • Pakistan's former environment minister used the AQLI to advocate for the National Clean Air Policy.

International organizations, including the World Health Organization and World Bank, have leveraged the AQLI to promote air quality initiatives. The index highlights particulate pollution, primarily from fossil fuels, as the world's leading threat to life expectancy.
